\subsection{Thermodynamics of a ternary mixture}
We begin by considering the a complex mixture of three components at constant temperature \(T\), Volume \(V\) and particle numbers \(N_A, N_B, N_C\). This system is best described by the Helmholtz free energy.
\(F=U-TS\).
For simplicity, we only consider an incompressible system with constant volume and molecular volume \(\nu\). In this case a vacancy caused by removing one particle must be filled by another particle. Thus we can introduce the relationship
\begin{equation}
    \phi_1 + \phi_2 + \phi_3 = 1
    \label{eq:incompressibility}
\end{equation}
where \(\phi_i = \frac{N_i \nu}{V}\) is the volume fraction of component \(i\). This relationship allows us to reduce the number of independent variables from three to two. Thus the free energy density can be written as \(f(\phi_1, \phi_2)\).
\subsubsection{Local stability analysis}
In a homogenous system the total free energy is equal to \(F = V f(\phi_1, \phi_2)\). The local stability of this system is determined by the curvature of the free energy landscape. In particular the system becomes unstable when the Hessian matrix of the free energy with respect to the volume fractions has a vanishing determinant. 
This is when the curvature in one changes sign and the system can lower its free energy by phase separating. To better understand why phase separating lowers the free energy it helps to take a closer look at the properties of concave functions.
In general a concave function \(f\) satisfies the property
\[ f(\lambda x_1 + (1-\lambda) x_2) \geq \lambda f(x_1) + (1-\lambda) f(x_2) \quad \text{for } \lambda \in [0,1] \]
For a system inside of the spinodal region the free energy density is concave in some direction in the \((\phi_1, \phi_2)\) plane, which implies
\[f(\bar{\boldsymbol\phi}) \geq \lambda f(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}) + (1-\lambda) f(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)})\]
where \(\bar{\boldsymbol\phi} = \lambda \boldsymbol\phi^{(1)} + (1-\lambda) \boldsymbol\phi^{(2)}\) and \(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}, \boldsymbol\phi^{(2)}\ll1\). This means that the system can lower its free energy by separating into two phases with volume fractions \(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}\) and \(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)}\) and relative volumes \(\lambda\) and \(1-\lambda\).
Note that this only works for systems where the interface region between the phases is much smaller than the bulk phases, otherwise the interfacial energy needs to be taken into account.
Since the spinodal is given by one condition it defines one or multiple lines in the \((\phi_1, \phi_2)\) plane and separates locally stable from unstable regions.

\subsubsection{Global stability analysis}
Local stability does not imply global stability. Although a system does not spontaneously phase separate there might still exist a phase separated state with lower free energy. To determine whether such a state exists we can examine the free energy of such a phase separated state. 
Again neglecting the interfacial energy, the free energy of a two phase system is given by
\begin{equation}
    V_1 f(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}) + V_2 f(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)}) 
    \label{eq:free_energy_two_phase}
\end{equation}
where \(V_1\) and \(V_2\) are the volumes of phase \(1\) and \(2\) respectively and \(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}\) and \(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)}\) are the corresponding volume fractions. The total volume and total number of particles need to be conserved, which yields additional equations
\( V_1 \phi_i^{(1)} + V_2 \phi_i^{(2)} = \bar\phi_i\) for \(i=1,2\) and \(V_1 + V_2 = V\). Therefore the six variables reduce to three independent variables.
Minimizing the free energy in Eq.~\ref{eq:free_energy_two_phase} with respect to these three variables yields the coexistence conditions
\[
    \begin{aligned}
        \frac{df(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)})}{d\phi_1} &= \frac{d f(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)})}{d\phi_1} \\
        \frac{df(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)})}{d\phi_2} &= \frac{d f(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)})}{d\phi_2} \\
        \phi_i^{(1)} \frac{df(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)})}{d\phi_i} - f(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}) &= \phi_i^{(2)} \frac{df(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)})}{d\phi_i} - f(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)}) 
    \end{aligned}
\]
Identifying the excess chemical potential \(\mu_i = \frac{df}{d\phi_i}\) and osmotic pressure \(\Pi = \phi_i \frac{df}{d\phi_i} - f\) we see that at coexistence the chemical potentials and osmotic pressures of both phases need to be equal.
\begin{subequations}
    \begin{align}
        \mu_1(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}) &= \mu_1(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)}) \\
        \mu_2(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}) &= \mu_2(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)}) \\
        \Pi(\boldsymbol\phi^{(1)}) &= \Pi(\boldsymbol\phi^{(2)})
    \end{align}
    \label{eq:coexistence_conditions}
\end{subequations}
The set of all points that satisfy these conditions define the binodal line in the \((\phi_1, \phi_2)\) plane.
Geometrically this condition can be interpreted as a common tangent construction in the free energy landscape. \textcolor{red}{Add figure here}
In general the spinodal will always be enclosed by the binodal, since local instability implies global instability.
Going the other way around is not necessarily true, since a system can be locally stable but globally unstable. This region between the binodal and spinodal lines is called metastable region.
For biological systems this means that in order to reach the global minimum the system needs to overcome an energy barrier. This can happen in the presence of thermal fluctuations, which can push the system over the barrier. This process is called nucleation but we will not discuss it further in this text.
When analysing properties of a system one can use the binodal and spinodal lines to approximate an upper and lower bound. As an example nucleation rates are bounded from below at the spinodal and from above at the binodal.

\subsubsection{Ising model}
We start by considering a one dimensional lattice model with three possible states at each lattice site \(1, 2, 3\) where the total number of each state is conserved.
Because we do not allow for vacant lattice cites we can reduce the number of states from three to two due to the condition:
\[\sigma_1 + \sigma_2 + \sigma_3 = 1\]
Interactions between lattice sites are restricted to nearest neighbors and have interaction energies \(\epsilon_{ij}\) where \(i,j \in \{1,2\}\).
Note that since we are investigating the reduced system the interaction energies \(\epsilon_{ij}\) have absorbed the interactions with the third component.\\
The full Hamiltonian has the form:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ising_hamiltonian}
H = \sum_n^N \epsilon_{11} \sigma_1^n \sigma_1^{n+1} + \epsilon_{22} \sigma_2^n \sigma_2^{n+1} + \epsilon_{12} (\sigma_1^n \sigma_2^{n+1} + \sigma_2^n \sigma_1^{n+1})
\end{equation}
Reducing the number of states introduces linear terms, but becuase the total number of each state is conserved these terms simply add a constant to the Energy and can thus be neglected.
\subsubsection{Mean-field and continuum limit}
Next we can rewrite the same-species and cross-species interactions in terms of single site and gradient expressions:
\[
\begin{aligned}
    2\sigma_j^n\sigma_j^{n+1} &=  (\sigma_j^n)^2 + (\sigma_j^{n+1})^2 - (\sigma_j^{n+1} - \sigma_j^n)^2  \\
        % &=  \sigma_j^n+ \sigma_j^{n+1}- (\sigma_j^{n+1} - \sigma_j^n)^2  \\
    \sigma_1^n \sigma_2^{n+1} + \sigma_2^n \sigma_1^{n+1} &= \sigma_1^n \sigma_2^n + \sigma_1^{n+1} \sigma_2^{n+1} - (\sigma_1^{n+1} - \sigma_1^n)(\sigma_2^{n+1} - \sigma_2^n)\\
\end{aligned}
\]
% In the second line we used the fact that \((\sigma_j^n)^2 = \sigma_j^n\)
We will also introduce the mean field approximation such that we can replace the lattice sites and their gradients by their average values \(\langle \sigma_j^n\rangle\) and \(\langle \sigma_j^{n+1}-\sigma_j^{n}\rangle\) respectively.
Lastly, we will take the continuum limit where the lattice spacing goes to zero, such that \(\langle \sigma_j^n \rangle \rightarrow \phi_j(x)\) and \(\langle \sigma_j^{n+1}-\sigma_j^{n}\rangle \rightarrow \nu^{1/3} \nabla \phi_j(x)\) where \(\nu\) is the lattice volume .
The internal Energy from Eq.~\ref{eq:ising_hamiltonian} now has the form: 
\[
    E =\nu^{-1/3}\int dx  \frac{1}{2}\phi_i(x)\epsilon_{ij}\phi_j(x) - \frac{\nu^{2/3}}{2}\nabla\phi_i(x) \epsilon_{ij} \nabla\phi_j(x)
\]
where we have introduced Einstein summation notation.
The entropy can be derived from combinatoric arguments and is given by: \textcolor{red}{Does this need a derivation, because I don't know how}
\[
\begin{aligned}
    TS = -\frac{k_B T}{\nu^{1/3}}\int& dx [ \phi_1(x) \ln(\phi_1(x)) + \phi_2(x) \ln(\phi_2(x))\\
    &+ (1-\phi_1(x)-\phi_2(x)) \ln(1-\phi_1(x)-\phi_2(x)) ]
\end{aligned}
\]
Therefore the free energy functional \(F = E - TS\) has the form
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fh_free_energy}
\begin{aligned}
    F[\phi_1, \phi_2] &= \frac{k_B T}{\nu^{1/3}} \int  dx  \frac{1}{2}\phi_i\chi_{ij}\phi_j  + \frac{1}{2}\nabla\phi_i \kappa_{ij} \nabla\phi_j\\
     &+\phi_1 \ln(\phi_1) + \phi_2 \ln(\phi_2) + (1-\phi_1-\phi_2) \ln(1-\phi_1-\phi_2)  
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Where \(\chi_{ij} = \frac{1}{k_B T}\epsilon_{ij}\) is the Flory-Huggins parameter matrix and \(\kappa_{ij} = -\frac{\nu^{2/3}}{k_B T}\epsilon_{ij}\) is the gradient coefficient matrix.
The free energy density \(f\) is thus given by:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
         \frac{f(\phi_1, \phi_2)}{k_B T\nu^{-1/3}} &=  \frac{1}{2}\phi_i\chi_{ij}\phi_j  + \frac{1}{2}\nabla\phi_i \kappa_{ij} \nabla\phi_j +\\
        &\phi_1 \ln(\phi_1) + \phi_2 \ln(\phi_2) + (1-\phi_1-\phi_2) \ln(1-\phi_1-\phi_2) 
    \end{aligned}
    \label{eq:fh_free_energy_density}
\end{equation}
Note that this is a line density and can be thought of as having absorbed the interfacial area.
As we can see the Flory-Huggins parameters and gradient coefficients are directly related to the interaction energies of the lattice model and thus not independent parameters.
At this point it is worth mentioning that there exists various approaches to derive the Flory-Huggins free energy that give different relationships between the Flory-Huggins parameters and the gradient coefficients and even depend on volume fractions.
Biological systems are nuanced and likely more complex than any of these theoretical models can capture.
The main purpose of this derivation is to illustrate that such a relationship can exist and to reduce the number of independent parameters.\\
In literature one will often find an additional parameter \(\Omega_i\) which is the molecular size of the components in units of the lattice size. Difference in molecular size will change the combinatorics of the system and thus the entropy contribution to the free energy.
We will not go into detail here since we assume all components to have the same molecular size.

\subsubsection{Interface stability}
Unlike the previous sections the free energy Eq.~\ref{eq:fh_free_energy} contains gradient terms. These terms penalize sharp interfaces and lead to stable interface profiles between coexisting phases.
One requirement for the \(\kappa\) matrix is that it needs to be positive definite. Otherwise the free energy could be lowered indefinitely by increasing gradients in certain directions.
This condition translates to \(\kappa_{11} > 0, \kappa_{22} > 0\) and \(|\kappa| > 0\). Because of the relationship between \(\kappa\) and \(\chi\) this also implies that \(\chi_{11} < 0, \chi_{22}<0\) and \(|\chi|<0|\).
Further is has implications on the allowed values of the interaction parameters in the full system \(\chi_{12}, \chi_{13}, \chi_{23}\).
The null-void interaction matrix is given by:
\[
    \chi = 
    \begin{pmatrix}
        - 2\chi_{10} & \chi_{12}-\chi_{10}-\chi_{20} \\
        \chi_{12}-\chi_{10}-\chi_{20} & -2\chi_{20}
    \end{pmatrix}
\]
It is easy to see that \(\chi_{10} > 0\) and \(\chi_{20} > 0\) are required for \(\chi_{11} < 0\) and \(\chi_{22} < 0\). The last condition \(|\chi| < 0\) translates to \[\chi_{12} \in [(\sqrt{\chi_{10}}-\sqrt{\chi_{20}})^2,(\sqrt{\chi_{10}}+\sqrt{\chi_{20}})^2]\]
Thus all interaction parameter need to be positive, which may come as a surprise since negative interaction parameters are often used to model attractive interactions.
One way to interpret this result is to look at the relative difference between interaction parameters. For example if \(\chi_{10}\) and \(\chi_{20}\) are large compared to \(\chi_{12}\) component \(1\) and \(2\) will effectively attract each other since they both repel the solvent strongly.
Additionally we will see that in our systems phase separation only occurs once the interaction parameter are above \(2\) such that the condition \(\chi_{ij}>0\) still allows for homogenous mixtures.
\subsection{Dynamics of phase separation}
In this section we will derive the laws that govern the dynamics of phase separation in ternary mixtures. We will start with the most general case and then make simplifications that are valid for the systems we are interested in.
The results are all thermodynamically consistent and ensure positive entropy production.
\subsubsection{Conservation Laws}
Due to mass conservation each component satisfies a continuity equation of the form
\[
    \frac{\partial \phi_i}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{J}_i = 0
\]
Where \(\boldsymbol{J}_i\) is the spacial flux of component \(i\). 
The flux can be separated into an advective part due to the center of mass velocity field \(\boldsymbol{v} = \sum_{i=0}^{N_c}\boldsymbol{J}_i\)
and a relative flux \(\boldsymbol{j}_i  =\phi_i(\boldsymbol{v}_i - \boldsymbol{v})\) driven by chemical potential gradients, where \(\boldsymbol{v}_i = \boldsymbol{J}_i/\phi_i\).
The continuity equation can thus be rewritten as
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \phi_i}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot (\phi_i \boldsymbol{v}) + \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{j}_i = 0
    \label{eq:continuity_equation}
\end{equation}
In the following we will restrict ourselves to systems where diffusion dominates over advection, i.e. the second term in Eq.~\ref{eq:continuity_equation} can be neglected.
This is valid for systems with low Peclet number \(Pe = \frac{Lv}{D} \ll 1\) where \(L\) is a characteristic length scale, \(v\) a characteristic velocity scale and \(D\) a characteristic diffusion coefficient.\\
To find the relationship between the relative fluxes and chemical potential gradients we can look at the entropy production of the system.
For a closed system at constant temperature and volume the change in entropy is related to the free energy change by \(\frac{dS}{dt} = -\frac{1}{T} \frac{dF}{dt}\).
Using the chain rule the time derivative of the free energy functional can be written as
\[\frac{dF}{dt} = \int d\boldsymbol{r} \frac{\delta f}{\delta \phi_i} \frac{\partial \phi_i}{\partial t} = \int d\boldsymbol{r} \bar\mu_i \frac{\partial \phi_i}{\partial t}\]
where we have identified the exchange chemical potential as the functional derivative of the free energy with respect to the volume fractions \(\bar\mu_i = \frac{\delta F}{\delta \phi_i}\).
Inserting the continuity equation and performing partial integration yields
\[\frac{dF}{dt} = \int d\boldsymbol{r} \nabla \bar\mu_i \cdot \boldsymbol{j}_i\]
where we have used that the fluxes vanish at the system boundaries. The entropy production is thus given by
\[\frac{dS}{dt} = -\frac{1}{T} \int d\boldsymbol{r} \nabla \bar\mu_i \cdot \boldsymbol{j}_i\]
which needs to be positive according to the second law of thermodynamics. The simplest linear relationship that ensures positive entropy production is given by
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{j}_i = - \sum_{i=1}^{N_c} \Lambda_{ij} \nabla \bar\mu_j
    \label{eq:flux_mobility}
\end{equation}
where \(\Lambda_{ij}\) is a positive definite mobility matrix of the reduced system. To due Onsager reciprocal relations the mobility matrix needs to be symmetric \(\Lambda_{ij} = \Lambda_{ji}\).
Inserting Eq.~\ref{eq:flux_mobility} into the continuity equation Eq.~\ref{eq:continuity_equation} yields the general form of the Cahn-Hilliard equations for a ternary mixture without hydrodynamics or thermal fluctuations:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \phi_i}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \left( \sum_{j=1}^{N_c} \Lambda_{ij}(\boldsymbol \phi) \nabla \bar\mu_j \right)
    \label{eq:cahn_hilliard_general}
\end{equation}
where in general the mobility matrix can depend on the local volume fractions.

\subsection{Surfacetension}
The surfacetension is a thermodynamic quantity which is related to the energy per area of the interface between two coexisting phases. It plays an important role when considering hydrodynamic effects or nucleation phenomena.
In order to compute the surface tension one must first set the interface profile between the two phases. For a binary system this is straightforward if one defines the inflection point of the profile as the interface location.
Then the surface tension is simply \(\gamma = F/A - x_I f(\phi^{(1)}) - (L-x_I) f(\phi^{(2)})\) where \(x_I\) is the inflection point and \(A\) the cross-sectional area of the system.
Unfortunately a ternary system does not generally have this symmetry in its interface profiles and thus no clear way to define the interface location. This forces one to think about the surface tension as a system property, rather than a geometric property.
Thus the surface tension should be the same quantity regardless of how we define the interface location.
In the following we will present two different approaches to derive the surface tension of a ternary system, once with a thermodynamic argument and once with a mechanical argument.
\subsubsection{Thermodynamic derivation}
We start by dividing the system into two bulk and one interface region. The free energy, entropy and the particle number of each region are given by.
\[
\begin{aligned}
    F &= F^{(1)}  + F^s+F^{(2)} \\
    S &= S^{(1)}  + S^s+S^{(2)} \\
    N_i &= N_i^{(1)}  + N_i^s+N_i^{(2)} 
\end{aligned}
\]
where \(s\) denotes the quantities assosiated with the interface and are known as excess quantitites.
The work done by a systems is given by 
\[dU = dQ + dW =  T dS + \sum_i \mu_i dN_i - p dV + \gamma dA\]
where the first two terms are the reversible heat received by the system and the chemical heat generated by the system respectively. The last two terms are the mechanical work \(dW\) done on the system.
Next we can write the total derivative of the free energy of the systems \(F = U - TS\)
\[ dF = -S dT - pdV + \gamma dA + \sum_i \mu_i dN_i\]
The surface area \(A\) is an interfacial quantity, such that we can rewrite the last equation in terms of excess quantities:
\[dF^s = -S^s dT  + \gamma dA + \sum_i \mu_i dN_i^s\]
notice that the volume is not an excess quantity.
if we now consider a process where we increase the area for constant temperature, volume and chemical potentials we find
\begin{equation}
    \gamma = \frac{1}{A}(F^s - \sum_i \mu_i \Gamma_i)
\end{equation}
where \(\Gamma_i = N_i-N_i^{(1)} - N_i^{(2)}\) is the excess particle number.
Therefore the main difference between the ternary and the binary case is that in the ternary case we need to consider the excess particle number in addition to the excess free energy. As a matter of fact the expression for the binary case is recovered when we select an interface location such that the excess particle number vanishes for all species, which in general is not possible to find for a ternary system.

\subsection{Numerical methods}
\subsubsection{Euler-Lagrange formalism}
In the previous sections we have derived the kinetics of phase separation from the general continuity equations and thermodynamic arguments. The resulting Cahn-Hilliard equations yield the equilibrium profiles by construction, but solving them numerically can be quite challenging due to the presence of fourth order derivatives.
Instead one can also derive the equilibrium profiles from variational calculus by minimizing the free energy functional with respect to the volume fractions \(\delta F/\delta \phi_i\) which yields the Euler-Lagrange equations:
\[
    \frac{\delta F[\phi_1, \phi_2]}{\delta \phi_i(x)} = \frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial \phi_i(x)} - \nabla \cdot \frac{\partial f(x)}{\partial \nabla \phi_i(x)} = 0
\]
Inserting the Flory-Huggins free energy density from Eq.~\ref{eq:fh_free_energy_density} yields a set of coupled second order PDEs for the volume fractions.
\[
    \begin{aligned}
        &\ln(\boldsymbol{\phi})
        - \ln\!\bigl(1-\mathbf{1}^\top\boldsymbol{\phi}\bigr)\,\mathbf{1}
        + \boldsymbol{\chi}\boldsymbol{\phi}
        - \boldsymbol{\kappa}\Delta\boldsymbol{\phi}
        = 0\\
        \Rightarrow\quad
        &\boldsymbol{\phi}
        = \boldsymbol{\chi}^{-1}
        \left(
        \boldsymbol{\kappa}\Delta\boldsymbol{\phi}
        - \ln(\boldsymbol{\phi})
        + \ln\!\bigl(1-\mathbf{1}^\top\boldsymbol{\phi}\bigr)\,\mathbf{1}
        \right)
        \eqcolon T(\boldsymbol{\phi})\\
    \end{aligned}
\]
Here and in the following, functions of vectors such as \(\ln(\boldsymbol\phi)\) are understood componentwise.

These equations can be solved using a fixed point iteration scheme.
Since \(T\) is generally not contractive, the naive iteration \(\boldsymbol \phi^{i+1} = T(\boldsymbol \phi^{i})\) may oscillate or diverge. We therefore use an explicit Euler step in an artificial (pseudo-)time variable with \(0 < h \leq 1\):
\[
    \boldsymbol{\phi}^{i+1}(x) = \boldsymbol{\phi}^{i}(x)+h \left[T(\boldsymbol \phi^{i}(x)) - \boldsymbol \phi^{i}(x)  \right] = \boldsymbol{\phi}^{i}(x)+h G(\boldsymbol \phi^{i}(x))
\]
where we have defined \(G(\boldsymbol \phi) = T(\boldsymbol \phi) - \boldsymbol \phi\).\\
To enforce mass conservation of each component at each step we modify the iteration scheme to:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\phi}^{i+1}(x) = \boldsymbol{\phi}^{i}(x)+h \left[G(\boldsymbol \phi^{i}(x))  - \langle G(\boldsymbol{\phi}^i(x)) \rangle_x\right]
    \label{eq:fixed_point_iteration}
\end{equation}
In principle, mass conservation could be enforced by introducing Lagrange multipliers in the free energy functional. Here, we instead assume the dynamics of the associated multipliers to be instantaneous, which leads to the projection onto the zero-mean subspace in Eq. \eqref{eq:fixed_point_iteration}.\\
Since the fixed points of \(G\) coincide with those of \(T\), the modified iteration converges to the same equilibrium solutions of the Euler–Lagrange equations.
Although the resulting dynamics are artificial and do not represent the physical time evolution, the scheme nevertheless converges to the same equilibrium profiles as the Cahn-Hilliard equations,while requiring the solution of only second order PDEs instead of fourth order ones.
\subsubsection{Pseudo-spectral method}
To solve Eq.~\ref{eq:fh_free_energy_density}, we employ a pseudo-spectral method, which is particularly efficient for fields governed by linear differential operators.
Unlike finite-difference or finite-element approaches, spectral methods evaluate spatial derivatives exactly in Fourier space, which reduces discretization error and allows high accuracy with relatively few grid points.
As a result, pseudo-spectral schemes often exhibit faster convergence and improved numerical stability for problems dominated by diffusive terms.

The central idea of the pseudo-spectral method is to use the Fourier transform to convert spatial derivatives into simple multiplications in Fourier space. In particular, second-order derivatives can be expressed as
\[
\partial_{xx}\boldsymbol{\phi}
= \mathcal{F}^{-1}\!\left[-k^2\,\mathcal{F}[\boldsymbol{\phi}]\right],
\]
where \(\mathcal{F}\) denotes the Fourier transform and \(k\) the wavevector. It allows us to evaluate the diffusive term in the operator \(G(\boldsymbol{\phi})\) efficiently in Fourier space, while nonlinear terms are computed in real space.

Since the pseudo-spectral method relies on Fourier transforms, periodic boundary conditions are required. These are implemented by mirroring the system at the right-hand boundary, ensuring that the dense phase is always surrounded by the dilute phase.


\subsubsection{Semi-implicit stepping scheme}
Solving PDEs using explicit schemes can be quite inefficient due to stability constraints on the time step size. 
For example the diffusion equation \(\dot \phi = D \phi_{xx}\) requires a time step size of \(\Delta t < \frac{\Delta x^2}{2D}\)

for stability when using an explicit Euler scheme. The instability arises from the high wavevector modes.
One trick of supressing these instabilities is to modify the discretized time derivate of \(\dot \phi\) by:
\[\frac{1}{h}(\boldsymbol{\phi}^{i+1}-\boldsymbol{\phi}^{i})  \rightarrow \left(\frac{1}{h} -\lambda \nabla^2\right) (\boldsymbol{\phi}^{i+1}-\boldsymbol{\phi}^{i})\]
where \(\lambda\) is a positive constant. 
Inserting this into the fixed point iteration scheme from Eq.~\ref{eq:fixed_point_iteration} and rearranging yields:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\phi}^{i+1} = \boldsymbol{\phi}^{i}+\frac{h}{1-h\lambda \nabla^2} \left[G(\boldsymbol \phi^{i})  - \langle G(\boldsymbol{\phi}^i) \rangle_x\right]
\end{equation}
From this we can see that high wavevector modes are supressed since \(\frac{1}{1+h\lambda k^2} \ll 1\) for large \(k\). This allows us to take much larger time steps without running into stability issues.
Note that for \(h \rightarrow 0\) the semi-implicit scheme reduces to the explicit scheme.

After having rescaled the free energy density in Eq.~\ref{eq:fh_free_energy_density} by \(k_B T \nu^{-1/3}\) the model only depends on the Flory-Huggins interaction matrix \(\chi_{ij}\)
and the gradient prefactor \(\lambda\). Since the gradient prefactor only sets the interfactial length scale, we fix \(\lambda=1\). 
With the convention used in Eq.~\eqref{eq:fh_free_energy_density}, the gradient coefficient matrix is then
\begin{equation}
    \kappa_{ij} = -\chi_{ij}.
\end{equation}

In the following, we restrict attention to interaction parameters \(\chi_{ij}\in[0,3]\).
This range reliably produces liquid--liquid phase separation,
while avoiding (as far as possible) regions of three-phase coexistence; see the
phase-diagram discussion in Sec.~\ref{sec:phase_diagram}.

For a chosen $\chi$-matrix, we initialize the system using the corresponding
coexisting bulk compositions. To reduce computational cost, these bulk phases are
computed first (using the \texttt{flory} package) and then used as boundary values
for the interfacial calculation. We initialize the interface as a sharp step
between the two coexisting phases.

\subsubsection{Equilibration dynamics and interfacial structure}

Starting from the initial step profile, we relax the system to equilibrium using the
fixed-point iteration in Eq.~\eqref{eq:fixed_point_iteration}. Since we are only interested in the equilibrium profiles,
the step size $h$ is chosen as large as possible while maintaining numerical stability. The semi-implicit update strongly
suppresses high-wavenumber modes, which allows us to use $h=\mathcal{O}(10^{-1})$,
typically one to two orders of magnitude larger than in a fully explicit scheme.


We declare convergence when the maximum change in the volume fractions
satisfies
\[
\max_x\, \lVert \boldsymbol{\phi}^{i+1}(x)-\boldsymbol{\phi}^{i}(x)\rVert < 10^{-10}.
\]
Depending on interaction parameters and initialization, this criterion is reached
after $10^3$--$10^4$ iterations.

We continue equilibrating the system for increased mean regulator concentrations until the critical point beyond which two-phase coexistence no longer is feasible.

At equilibrium, the droplet-solvent interface exhibits a smooth, sigmoid-like transition in each component.
The regulator typically accumulates at the interface, consistent with its role in reducing the energetic penalty of direct droplet-solvent
contacts (i.e.\ screening the strongest repulsive interactions).
Interestingly the degree of absorption at the interface increases as the repulsive interactions between regulator and the other two components increases and can even lead to the formation of three-phase coexistence.
For a symmetric case \(\chi_{dr} = \chi_{rs}\), the regulator profile
is symmetric about the interface. In this regime, Huang and Olvera de la Cruz showed
that the interfacial adsorption scales linearly with the ratio
$\chi_{\mathrm{dr}}/\chi_{\mathrm{ds}}$, which is consistent with the trends observed
in our simulations.

\subsubsection{Critical scaling of the surface tension}
Once the equilibrium profiles are obtained, the surface tension is
computed using the thermodynamic relation derived in the previous section. The
resulting surface tensions are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:surface_tension_numerical}.


As the system approaches the critical point, the interface continuously vanishes, which explains why the surface tension vanishes as well.

Close to criticality, we observe a clear power-law dependence of the surface
tension on the distance from the critical point, which we quantify by the chemical
potential difference $\mu_R-\mu_{R,c}$:
\[
\gamma \sim (\mu_R-\mu_{R,c})^{\delta}.
\]
This scaling is observed for a wide range of interaction parameters. By fitting
the numerical data, we extract the corresponding critical exponent $\delta$ for
each parameter set. The results are summarized in Fig.~\ref{fig:critical_exponent_numerical}.

Within numerical uncertainty, the critical exponent is universal across all
interaction parameters considered and takes a value $\delta \approx 3/2$. This
is consistent with the classical mean-field prediction for binary mixtures
derived by Cahn~\cite{Cahn1958FreeTE}. In contrast, the prefactor of the power law
is not universal and varies substantially with the choice of interaction
parameters, reflecting non-universal microscopic details of the system.

Remarkably, the observed power-law behavior persists over a broad range of
$\mu_R-\mu_{R,c}$ values.

\subsection{An effective interfacial model to approximate the surface tension}

While the critical scaling of the surface tension near the critical point provides
important physical insight, obtaining a closed-form expression for $\gamma$ from
the full ternary Flory-Huggins free energy is generally intractable. Consequently,
for each choice of interaction parameters and mean regulator concentration, the surface
tension must be computed by solving the full numerical interfacial problem.

To avoid this computational cost, we now introduce an analytical approximation that
estimates the surface tension using only the bulk volume fractions of the coexisting
phases and the interaction parameters. The approximation remains
quantitatively accurate over a broad range of interacrtion parameters and has
the correct scaling near the critical point, as we will demonstrate later.

\subsubsection{Derivation of a piecewise-linear interface}
As shown in the previous sections, interfacial profiles in ternary mixtures can be
highly nontrivial. To make progress, we approximate the interface by a
piecewise-linear profile that connects the two coexisting bulk phases over a finite
interfacial width.


This approximation neglects two important features of the exact solution. First, it
assumes a common interfacial width for all components, which excludes
adsorption of any species at the interface. Second, it ignores the intrinsic
nonlinearity of the equilibrium profiles. Despite these simplifications, the
piecewise-linear ansatz allows for a closed-form expression for the surface tension.

To begin, we note that for a one-dimensional, planar interface at equilibrium, the
Euler–Lagrange equations imply the existence of a conserved quantity across the
interface. Writing the free-energy density as a sum of a local and a gradient
contribution and adopting Einstein summation convention, we define
\begin{equation}
    f(x)
    = f_{\mathrm{local}}(\{\phi_i(x)\})
    + \frac{1}{2}\,\nabla \phi_i(x)\,\kappa_{ij}\,\nabla \phi_j(x).
\end{equation}
Introducing the exchange chemical potentials $\mu_i$ and osmotic pressure $\Pi$, the
combination
\begin{equation}
    f_{\mathrm{local}}(\{\phi_i(x)\})
    - \mu_i \phi_i(x)
    + \Pi
    - \frac{1}{2}\,\nabla \phi_i(x)\,\kappa_{ij}\,\nabla \phi_j(x)
    \eqcolon -V(\{\phi_i\}) - T(\{\nabla \phi_i\})
    \label{eq:invariant_quantity}
\end{equation}
is invariant across the interface. Here, the terms $V$ and $T$ play roles analogous
to potential and kinetic energy in classical mechanics.

Taking the spatial derivative of Eq.~\eqref{eq:invariant_quantity} yields
\[
\begin{aligned}
    \frac{d}{dx} \Bigl(
    f_{\mathrm{local}}(\{\phi_i\})
    - \mu_i \phi_i
    + \Pi
    - \frac{1}{2}\,\nabla \phi_i\,\kappa_{ij}\,\nabla \phi_j
    \Bigr)
    &=
    \left(
    \frac{\partial f_{\mathrm{local}}}{\partial \phi_i}
    - \mu_i
    - \kappa_{ij} \Delta \phi_j
    \right)
    \frac{d\phi_i}{dx} \\
    &= 0,
\end{aligned}
\]
where the expression in parentheses vanishes by virtue of the Euler–Lagrange
equations. Consequently, the quantity in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:invariant_quantity} is constant throughout the interfacial region.


For our approximation we assume that each component varies linearly across an interface of width $w$,
connecting the bulk values $\phi_i^{(1)}$ and $\phi_i^{(2)}$. The magnitude of the
gradient is therefore approximated by
\[
    |\nabla \phi_i| \simeq \frac{|\Delta \phi_i|}{w},
\]
where $\Delta \phi_i = \phi_i^{(2)} - \phi_i^{(1)}$ denotes the difference between the
coexisting bulk phases. The profile is centered around the mean composition
\[
    \bar{\phi}_i = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\phi_i^{(1)} + \phi_i^{(2)}\right),
\]
at which the local free-energy density is evaluated. Since we assume a common
interfacial width for all components, this approximation holds for all
$i$.
Substituting the gradient approximation into the quadratic gradient term
yields
\[
     \nabla \bar{\phi}_i\, \kappa_{ij}\, \nabla \bar{\phi}_j
    =
    w^{-2}\,\Delta \phi_i\, \kappa_{ij}\, \Delta \phi_j.
\]
Solving for the interfacial width gives
\[
    w
    =
    \sqrt{
    \frac{
        \Delta \phi_i\, \kappa_{ij}\, \Delta \phi_j
    }{
        \nabla \bar{\phi}_i\, \kappa_{ij}\, \nabla \bar{\phi}_j
    }
    }.
\]


The denominator can be expressed using the invariant quantity
Eq.~\eqref{eq:invariant_quantity}, evaluated at the center of the interface. This leads to the
approximate expression
\begin{equation}
    w
    =
    \sqrt{
    \frac{
        \Delta \phi_i\, \kappa_{ij}\, \Delta \phi_j
    }{
        2\left(
        f_{\mathrm{local}}(\{\bar{\phi}_i\})
        - \mu_i \bar{\phi}_i
        + \Pi
        \right)
    }}
    \label{eq:interface_width_approximation}
\end{equation}

The surface tension corresponding to the piecewise-linear interfacial profile is
obtained by integrating the gradient contribution to the free energy,
\[
    \gamma
    =
    \int dx\, \nabla \phi_i\, \kappa_{ij}\, \nabla \phi_j.
\]
Within the linear-profile approximation, the gradients are constant across the
interface and vanish in the bulk. The integral therefore reduces to
\[
    \gamma
    =
    w\, \nabla \bar{\phi}_i\, \kappa_{ij}\, \nabla \bar{\phi}_j,
\]
Substituting the expression for $w$ from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:interface_width_approximation} yields the approximate surface tension
\begin{equation}
    \gamma
    =
    \sqrt{
    2\left(
        \Delta \phi_i\, \kappa_{ij}\, \Delta \phi_j
    \right)
    \left(
        f_{\mathrm{local}}(\{\bar{\phi}_i\})
        - \mu_i \bar{\phi}_i
        + \Pi
    \right)
    }
    \label{eq:surface_tension_approximation}
\end{equation}

As anticipated, this expression depends only on the bulk compositions
$\{\phi_i^{(1)}, \phi_i^{(2)}\}$ of the coexisting phases and the interaction
parameters through $\kappa_{ij}$. Importantly, the closed-form nature of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:surface_tension_approximation} allows derivatives of the surface
tension with respect to order parameters to be evaluated analytically.

\subsubsection{Comparison between analytical approximation and numerical results}
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/st_approx.png}
    \caption{Exact (blue) and approximate (red) surface tension as a function of the chemical potential for various interaction parameters. 
    For reference, the scaling behavior with exponent \(1.5\) is shown as a dashed line.}
    \label{fig:surface_tension}
\end{figure}

The quantitative comparison between the analytical approximation and the numerical
results is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:surface_tension}. Somewhat surprisingly, the
approximate expression not only reproduces the correct critical scaling but also
provides good quantitative agreement with the numerically computed surface tension
over a wide range of parameters. Depending on the interaction matrix the surface tension will vary \(10\%\) to \(70\%\) from the exact value.

A systematic deviation is nevertheless apparent: the analytical approximation
consistently overestimates the exact surface tension. This behavior can be
understood naturally within the variational formulation of the interfacial problem.
Using the invariant quantity from Eq.~\eqref{eq:invariant_quantity}, the effective
Lagrangian density of the system can be identified as

\[
    L = T - V = 2T = \nabla \phi_i \,\kappa_{ij}\, \nabla \phi_j,
\]


where $T$ and $V$ denote the gradient and local contributions, respectively. The
surface tension can therefore be interpreted as the action associated with the
interfacial profile,
\[
    \gamma = \int dx\, L \equiv S.
\]

Since the true equilibrium interface minimizes this action, any approximate
interfacial profile necessarily yields a larger value of $\gamma$. The systematic
overestimation observed in the analytical approximation is thus a direct
consequence of the underlying variational principle.

\subsubsection{Asymptotic critical scaling}
\textcolor{red}{Either use +/- or 1/2 for dense/dilute phase but not both. Also the derivation would benefit from a schematic figure showing the tie line, critical point and deviations in phi space.}


In the previous section, we showed that the surface tension can be expressed
approximately in terms of the bulk compositions of the coexisting phases. We now
analyze the behavior of this expression in the vicinity of the critical point, with
the goal of deriving its asymptotic scaling form.

Recall that the approximate surface tension is given by
Eq.~\eqref{eq:surface_tension_approximation} as the product of two factors. We first
consider the term
\[
    \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta \phi_i \,\kappa_{ij}\, \Delta \phi_j,
\]
which depends solely on the difference between the bulk compositions of the two
coexisting phases.

Close to the critical point, the volume fractions in the dense $(+)$ and dilute $(-)$
phases can be written as small deviations from the critical composition
$\phi_{i,c}$,
\[
    \phi_i^{(\pm)} = \phi_{i,c} \pm\tfrac{1}{2} \Delta \phi_i,
\]
where $\Delta \phi_i$ parameterizes the displacement of the coexisting phases along
the tie line in composition space.

% \[

%     =
%     \phi_i^{(+)} - \phi_i^{(-)}
%     =
%     2\,\delta \phi_i.
% \]

% Substituting this expression into the quadratic form yields
% \[
%     \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta \phi_i \,\kappa_{ij}\, \Delta \phi_j
%     =
%     2\, \delta \phi_i \,\kappa_{ij}\, \delta \phi_j.
% \]
% Thus, the scaling of this term near criticality is entirely determined by the

Expanding the chemical potentials in the two coexisting phases about the critical
point yields
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(\pm)} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_c
    =
    \boldsymbol{H}\,\Delta\boldsymbol{\phi}^{(\pm)}
    + \mathcal{O}(\Delta \phi^2),
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{H}$ denotes the Hessian of the local free-energy density evaluated
at the critical composition, and
$\Delta\boldsymbol{\phi}^{(\pm)} = \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(\pm)} - \boldsymbol{\phi}_c$.

At coexistence, the chemical potentials in the two phases must be equal,
$\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(+)}=\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(-)}$. Subtracting the two expansions and
defining
\[
    \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}
    =
    \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(+)} - \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(-)},
\]
we obtain, to leading order,
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{H}\,\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi} = 0 .
\end{equation}
This shows that the difference between the bulk compositions of the coexisting
phases lies in the null space of the Hessian at the critical point.

Furthermore, in the immediate vicinity of the critical point, the binodal is
approximately linear. Consequently, the deviations
$\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(\pm)}$ are parallel to $\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}$ and
thus also belong to the null space of $\boldsymbol{H}$. As a result, the linear term
in the Taylor expansion of the chemical potentials vanishes along the coexistence
direction, and higher-order terms control the leading behavior near criticality.


We now turn to the leading nonvanishing contribution in the Taylor expansion of the
chemical potential about the critical point. Expanding to second order yields
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\mu}^{(\pm)} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_c
    =
    \frac{1}{2}
    \left(\alpha^{(\pm)}\right)^2
    \left[
        \frac{\partial^2 \mu_i}{\partial \phi_j \partial \phi_k}
        \,\Delta \phi_j \Delta \phi_k
    \right]\boldsymbol{e}_i
    + \mathcal{O}(\Delta \phi^3),
    \label{eq:second_order}
\end{equation}
where all derivatives are evaluated at the critical composition, and we have
introduced
\[
    \alpha^{(\pm)}
    =
    \frac{\lvert \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(\pm)} \rvert}
         {\lvert \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi} \rvert}.
\]

Since the chemical potentials must be equal in the two coexisting phases,
$\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(+)}=\boldsymbol{\mu}^{(-)}$, the prefactors must satisfy
$\left(\alpha^{(+)}\right)^2=\left(\alpha^{(-)}\right)^2$. The two phases are
distinguished by opposite displacements along the tie line, which implies
\[
    \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(+)}
    =
    -\,\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(-)}
    =
    \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}.
\]

The tensor contraction in brackets in Eq.~\eqref{eq:second_order} is generically
nonzero. Consequently, the leading dependence of the chemical potential deviation on
the distance from the critical point is
\[
    \lvert \boldsymbol{\mu}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_c \rvert
    \sim
    \lvert \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi} \rvert^2 .
\]
\footnote{The chemical potential remains constant along the tie line; therefore, it
only depends on the magnitude $\lvert \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi} \rvert$ and not on the
direction in composition space.}

Finally, since $\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}$ lies in the null space of the Hessian at the
critical point, the leading nontrivial contribution to the dot product
$(\boldsymbol{\mu}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_c)^T \Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}$ is equal to the
third derivative of the free energy. This quantity vanishes exactly at the critical
point, implying that $\boldsymbol{\mu}-\boldsymbol{\mu}_c$ is orthogonal to the tie
lines in $(\phi_D,\phi_R)$ composition space.

We now turn to the second factor in the expression for the surface tension,
\begin{equation}
    \nabla \bar{\phi}_i \,\kappa_{ij}\, \nabla \bar{\phi}_j
    =
    f(\bar{\boldsymbol{\phi}})
    - \mu_i \bar{\phi}_i
    + \Pi
    =
    f(\bar{\boldsymbol{\phi}})
    - f(\boldsymbol{\phi}^{(+)})
    + \mu_i \bigl(\phi_i^{(+)} - \bar{\phi}_i\bigr),
    \label{eq:second_product}
\end{equation}
where, in the first equality, we have used the conservation relation
Eq.~\eqref{eq:invariant_quantity}.

Close to the critical point, the dense-phase composition can be written as
\[
    \phi_i^{(+)} = \phi_{i,c} + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\Delta \phi_i ,
\]
while the midpoint composition satisfies
\[
    \bar{\phi}_i
    =
    \tfrac{1}{2}\bigl(\phi_i^{(+)} + \phi_i^{(-)}\bigr)
    =
    \phi_{i,c},
\]
where the last equality follows from the symmetry
$\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(+)} = -\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}^{(-)}$.

We now expand the local free-energy density about the critical composition
$\boldsymbol{\phi}_c$. Using Einstein summation convention, we obtain
\begin{equation}
    \begin{split}
        f(\boldsymbol{\phi}^{(+)})
        &=
        f(\boldsymbol{\phi}_c)
        + \frac{1}{2}\,\mu_{i,c}\,\Delta \phi_i
        + \frac{1}{2^2\,2!}\,
        \Delta \phi_i \underbrace{H_{ij}\,\Delta \phi_j}_{=\,0} \\
        &\quad
        + \frac{1}{2^3\,3!}\,
        \underbrace{
        \frac{\partial^3 f}{\partial \phi_i \partial \phi_j \partial \phi_k}
        \Delta \phi_i \Delta \phi_j \Delta \phi_k
        }_{=\,0} \\
        &\quad
        + \frac{1}{2^4\,4!}\,
        \frac{\partial^4 f}{\partial \phi_i \partial \phi_j \partial \phi_k \partial \phi_l}
        \Delta \phi_i \Delta \phi_j \Delta \phi_k \Delta \phi_l
        + \mathcal{O}(\Delta \phi^5).
    \end{split}
\end{equation}

The quadratic term vanishes because $\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}$ lies in the null space
of the Hessian at the critical point. The cubic term vanishes because the third
derivative of the free energy along the coexistence direction is zero at criticality.
Consequently, the leading nonzero contribution to the second factor in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:second_product} arises from the quartic term, which is generically
finite.

The chemical potential can then be written as
\begin{equation}
   \tfrac{1}{2}\mu_i\Delta \phi_i
   =\tfrac{1}{2}\mu_{i,c}\Delta \phi_i
    + \frac{1}{2^4 \cdot 3!}\,
     \frac{\delta^4 f}{\delta \phi_i \delta \phi_j \delta \phi_k \delta \phi_l}\,
     \Delta \phi_j \Delta \phi_k \Delta \phi_l
   + \mathcal{O}(\Delta \phi^5) .
\end{equation}

Collecting terms, we find
\begin{equation}
    f(\boldsymbol{\phi}_c) - f(\boldsymbol{\phi}^{(+)}) 
    + \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mu_i \Delta \phi_i 
    = \frac{1}{2^7}\,
      \frac{\delta^4 f}{\delta \phi_i \delta \phi_j \delta \phi_k \delta \phi_l}\,
      \Delta \phi_i \Delta \phi_j \Delta \phi_k \Delta \phi_l
    + \mathcal{O}(\Delta \phi^5) .
\end{equation}

This shows that Eq.~\eqref{eq:second_product} scales as 
\[
    |\Delta \boldsymbol{\phi}|^4 \;\propto\; |\boldsymbol{\mu} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_c|^2 .
\]
Combining this result with the scaling of the first factor, we conclude that near the critical point the surface tension obeys
\[
    \gamma \;\propto\; |\boldsymbol{\mu} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_c|^{3/2} .
\]

In addition, this analysis provides the scaling of the interfacial width:
\[
    w 
    = \frac{\sqrt{\Delta \phi_i \,\kappa_{ij}\,\Delta \phi_j}}
           {\sqrt{\nabla \bar{\phi}_i \,\kappa_{ij}\,\nabla \bar{\phi}_j}}
    \;\propto\; |\boldsymbol{\mu} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_c|^{-1/2} .
\]
Which diverges at the critical point, as expected.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figures/scaling_behavior.png}
    \caption{Numerical scaling behavior of the first (bottom) and second (top) product terms in Eq. \ref{eq:surface_tension_approximation}, as a function of the chemical potential.}
    \label{fig:scaling}
\end{figure}

Taken together, these results provide a direct connection between the bulk
thermodynamic framework developed in the previous chapter and the interfacial
properties analyzed here. The critical exponents
$\gamma \sim |\mu-\mu_c|^{3/2}$ and $w \sim |\mu-\mu_c|^{-1/2}$ arise naturally from
the mean-field structure of the Flory-Huggins free energy, in which the critical
point is characterized by a vanishing curvature of the bulk free-energy
along the coexistence direction. In this sense the
effective interfacial model does not introduce new critical behavior, but rather
demonstrate how the bulk critical scaling discussed earlier manifests itself in
interfacial observables such as the surface tension and the interface width.

\subsubsection{Sources of error and limitations}

The piecewise-linear approximation differs from the true equilibrium interfacial
profiles in two essential ways. First, it replaces the smooth, nonlinear variation
of the volume fractions across the interface by a linear interpolation. Second, it
assumes that all species share a common interfacial width, thereby neglecting
preferential adsorption.

As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:surface_tension}, the approximation performs best far from
the critical point, where the mean regulator concentration becomes small and the
system effectively reduces to a binary mixture. In this regime, the two remaining
components indeed exhibit nearly identical interfacial widths, and the dominant
source of error arises from the simplified functional form of the profile. If one
assumes that this contribution varies only weakly across phase space, this
observation suggests that deviations from the equal-width assumption constitute the
primary source of error closer to criticality.

To quantify the importance of interfacial adsorption, we fit a hyperbolic tangent
profile of the form
\[
    \phi(x)
    =
    \frac{\Delta \phi}{2}\,
    \tanh\!\left(\frac{x - x_0}{w}\right)
    + \bar{\phi}
\]
to the numerical equilibrium profiles of both the droplet and solvent components.
The difference between the fitted interface positions $x_0$ for the two species then
provides a direct measure of the deviation from the equal-width assumption and thus
of the degree of interfacial adsorption.

\section{Expanding ternary interfacial profile}
For interaction matrix
\[
    \begin{pmatrix}
         \chi_d & \chi_o \\
        \chi_o & \chi_d
    \end{pmatrix}
\]
and \(\kappa_{ij} = -\chi_{ij}\) we have EL equations
\[
\begin{aligned}
    &-\chi_d\Delta \phi_1 - \chi_o \Delta \phi_2 = \chi_d \phi_1 + \chi_o \phi_2 + \ln(\phi_1) - \ln(1-\phi_1-\phi_2)-\mu_1\\
    &-\chi_o\Delta \phi_1 - \chi_d \Delta \phi_2 = \chi_o \phi_1 + \chi_d \phi_2 + \ln(\phi_2) - \ln(1-\phi_1-\phi_2)-\mu_2\\
\end{aligned}
\]
Where \(\mu_1=\mu_2=\mu=\chi_d\phi_1^{(1)} + \chi_o \phi_2^{(1)} + \ln(\phi_1^{(1)}) - \ln(\phi_0^{(1)})\) is the chemical potential of the coexisting phases.
Define \(\phi_\pm = \tfrac{1}{2}(\phi_1 \pm \phi_2)\) such that we can rewrite the EL equations as
\[
\begin{aligned}
    &\frac{2}{\bar\phi^*}\Delta \phi_+ = -\frac{2}{\bar\phi^*}\phi_+ + \ln(\phi_+ + \phi_-)+\ln(\phi_+ - \phi_-) -2 \ln(1-2\phi_+) - 2\mu \\
    &\frac{2}{\bar\phi_c}\Delta \phi_- = -\frac{2}{\bar\phi_c}\phi_- + \ln(\phi_+ + \phi_-)-\ln(\phi_+ - \phi_-)\\
\end{aligned}
\]
Where we use \(\chi_o - \chi_d = 1/\bar\phi_c\) and \(\chi_o+\chi_d = -1/\bar\phi^*\).\\
The chemical potential can be rewritten in terms of the mean volume fraction \(\bar\phi=(\phi_1^{(1)}+\phi_2^{(2)})/2\) and the difference 
\(\Delta\phi = (\phi_1^{(1)}-\phi_2^{(2)})/2\)
\[\mu=-\bar\phi/\phi^* - \Delta\phi/\phi_c+\ln(\bar\phi+\Delta\phi)-\ln(1-2\bar\phi)\].
First we will focus on the EL equation for \(\phi_-\).
For this we can look at the equation in the bulk where the laplacian is zero and \(\phi_+ = \bar\phi\) is constant.
Then we get the implicit equation for the bulk value of \(\phi_-(x\rightarrow\infty) = \Delta \phi\):
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    &-\frac{2}{\bar\phi_c}\Delta \phi + \ln(\bar\phi + \Delta\phi)-\ln(\bar\phi - \Delta\phi) = 0\\
    \Rightarrow\quad&\Delta\delta \phi = \bar\phi\tanh\left(\frac{\Delta\phi}{\phi_c}\right)\\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Next we will look at the EL equation for \(\phi_+\) at the interface. Due to anti-symmetry of \(\phi_-\) around the interface center we have \(\phi_-(x=0)=0\) and therefore
\[
\begin{aligned}
    &\frac{2}{\bar\phi^*}\Delta \phi_+ = -\frac{2}{\bar\phi^*}\phi_+ + 2\ln(\phi_+) - 2 \ln(1-2\phi_+) - 2\mu \\
    \Rightarrow\quad&\frac{\Delta \phi_+}{\bar\phi^*} = -\frac{\delta\phi_+}{\bar\phi^*} + \frac{\Delta\phi}{\phi_c} + \ln\left(\frac{\bar\phi+\delta\phi_+}{\bar\phi+\Delta\phi}\right)+\ln\left(\frac{1-2\bar\phi}{1-2(\bar\phi+\delta\phi_+}\right) \\
\end{aligned}
\]
Where we have defined \(\delta\phi_+ = \phi_+ - \bar\phi\).
Now we can rearrange this to the implicit equation for \(\delta\phi_+\):
\begin{equation}
    \delta \phi_+ =\frac{1-2\bar\phi}{2}\left(1- \frac{1}{1-2\bar\phi+2(\bar\phi+\Delta\phi)\Pi}\right)
\end{equation}
    % \delta \phi_+ = \frac{1-2\bar\phi}{2}\frac{-2\bar\phi+2(\bar\phi+\Delta\phi)\Pi}{1-2\bar\phi+2(\bar\phi+\Delta\phi)\Pi}=\frac{1-2\bar\phi}{2}\left(1- \frac{1}{1-2\bar\phi+2(\bar\phi+\Delta\phi)\Pi}\right)
Where we have defined 
\begin{equation}
    \Pi = \exp\left(\frac{\delta \phi_+}{\bar\phi^*}(1+a_2) - \frac{\Delta \phi}{\phi_c}\right)
\end{equation}
Here \(a_2 = \Delta\delta\phi_+/\delta\phi_+\) is the curvature of \(\phi_+\) at the interface center.
If we assume \(a_2\ll1\) we can solve this equation self-consistently for \(\delta\phi_+\) after having solved for \(\Delta\phi\).
This assumption is correct near the critical point as here the interface width diverges 
\[
\Delta \delta\phi_+ \approx \frac{\delta\phi_+(\epsilon) - 2\delta\phi_+(0) + \delta\phi_+(-\epsilon)}{\epsilon^2} \sim \frac{\delta\phi_+(0)}{w^2} \ll \delta\phi_+
\]
The results can be seen in figure~\ref{fig:phi_plus_minus_comparison}.
\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{figures/delphipl.png}
    \caption{Comparison between numerical solution of EL equations (solid lines) and analytical approximation (dashed lines) for \(\chi_{12}=2.3\).}
    \label{fig:phi_plus_minus_comparison}
\end{figure}
Next we can look at the interface width, \(w = \Delta \phi / \nabla \phi_{-,IF}\), where \(\nabla \phi_{-,IF}\) is the gradient of \(\phi_-\) at the interface center.
At the interface we have \(\phi_- = \nabla\phi_+ = 0\). We can use the conserved quantity from Eq.~\ref{eq:invariant_quantity} to rewrite this as
\[
    f - 2\mu \phi_+ + \Pi = 2\phi_+\ln(\phi_+)+(1-2\phi_+)\ln(1-2\phi_+)-\frac{\phi_+^2}{\bar\phi^*}-2\mu\phi_++\Pi=\frac{(\nabla\phi_-)^2}{\bar\phi_c}
\]
and thus:
\begin{equation}
    w = \sqrt{\frac{\bar\phi_c (\Delta \phi)^2}{2\phi_+\ln(\phi_+)+(1-2\phi_+)\ln(1-2\phi_+)-\frac{\phi_+^2}{\bar\phi^*}-2\mu\phi_++\Pi}}
\end{equation}
The analytical approximation for the interface width can be seen in figure~\ref{fig:interface_width_comparison}.
Here we can see that including the absorption yields a much better agreement with the numerical results.

\end{figure}

\subsection{Finding \(\phi_-\)}
In order to get \(\phi_-\) we can expand the logarithms for \(\phi_+ = \bar \phi + \delta \phi_+\), where \(\delta \phi_+, \phi_- \ll \bar\phi\).
\[
    \begin{aligned}
    \ln(\phi_+ \pm \phi_-) &= \ln(\bar\phi) + \frac{\delta \phi_+}{\bar\phi} \pm \frac{\phi_-}{\bar\phi} - \frac{\phi_-^2}{2\bar\phi^2} \mp \frac{\delta \phi_+ \phi_-}{\bar\phi^2}-\frac{(\delta\phi_+)^2}{2\bar\phi^2}\\
        &+\frac{\delta\phi_+(\phi_-)^2}{\bar\phi^3} \pm \frac{\phi_-^3}{3\bar\phi^3}+\frac{\phi_-^4}{4\bar\phi^4} + \mathcal{O}((a+m)^5)
    \end{aligned}
\]
and therefore:
\[
    \ln(\phi_+ + \phi_-)-\ln(\phi_+ - \phi_-) \approx  2\left(\frac{ \phi_-}{\bar\phi} - \frac{\delta \phi_+\phi_-}{\bar\phi^2} +\frac{\phi_-^3}{3\bar\phi^3} \right)
\]
Inserting this into the EL for \(\phi_-\) yields:
\[
\begin{aligned}
    &\frac{2\Delta \phi_-}{\bar\phi_c} = -\frac{2}{\bar\phi_c} \phi_- +  \frac{ 2}{\bar\phi} \phi_- - \frac{2}{\bar\phi^2}\delta \phi_+ \phi_- + \frac{2}{3\bar\phi^3}\phi_-^3 \\
    \Rightarrow\quad&\Delta \phi_- =  \left(\frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi} - 1\right) \phi_- - \frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi^2}\delta \phi_+ \phi_- + \frac{\bar\phi_c}{3\bar\phi^3}\phi_-^3 \\
\end{aligned}
\]
The laplacian of \(\phi_- = a \tanh(kx)\) is given by
\[
    \Delta \phi_- = -2 k^2 a\tanh(kx) + 2 k^2 a \tanh^3(kx)
\]
On the other hand if we insert the ansatz in the right hand side we get
\[
\begin{aligned}
    &\left(\frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi} - 1\right) a \tanh(kx) - \frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi^2} m \sech^2(kx) a \tanh(kx)\\
    & + \frac{\bar\phi_c}{3\bar\phi^3} a^3 \tanh^3(kx) \\
    =&\left(\frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi} - 1 - \frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi^2} m \right) a \tanh(kx) + \left(\frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi^2}m +\frac{\bar\phi_c}{3\bar\phi^3} a^2\right) a\tanh^3(kx) \\
\end{aligned}
\]
By comparing the LHS and RHS we get the relationships
\[
    \begin{aligned}
    &2k^2 = -\frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi} + 1 + \frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi^2} m = \frac{\bar\phi_c}{\bar\phi^2}m +\frac{\bar\phi_c}{3\bar\phi^3} a^2\\
    \Rightarrow\quad&a = \sqrt{\frac{3\bar\phi}{\bar\phi_c}(\bar\phi-\bar\phi_c)}
    \end{aligned}
\]
\subsection{Finding \(\phi_+\)}
Next we will investigate the equation for \(\phi_+\). For this we expand the logarithms as before:
\[
    \begin{aligned}
    \ln(\phi_+ \pm \phi_-) &= \ln(\bar\phi) + \frac{\delta \phi_+}{\bar\phi} \pm \frac{\phi_-}{\bar\phi} - \frac{(\delta \phi_+)^2}{2\bar\phi^2} - \frac{\phi_-^2}{2\bar\phi^2} \mp \frac{\delta \phi_+ \phi_-}{\bar\phi^2}\\
        &+\frac{(\delta\phi_+)^3}{3\bar\phi^3}+\frac{\delta\phi_+(\phi_-)^2}{\bar\phi^3}\pm\frac{(\delta \phi_+)^2\phi_-}{\bar\phi^3} \pm \frac{\phi_-^3}{3\bar\phi^3} + \mathcal{O}((\delta \phi_+ + \phi_-)^4)
    \end{aligned}
\]

\[
    \ln(\phi_+ + \phi_-)+\ln(\phi_+ - \phi_-) = 2\ln(\bar\phi) + \frac{2\delta \phi_+}{\bar\phi} - \frac{(\delta \phi_+)^2}{\bar\phi^2} - \frac{\phi_-^2}{\bar\phi^2}+\frac{2\delta\phi_+(\phi_-)^2}{\bar\phi^3}-\frac{\phi_-^4}{2\bar\phi^4}  \mathcal{O}((\delta \phi_+\pm \phi_-)^4)
\]
We can expand also \(\ln(1-2\phi_+)\) around \(\bar\phi\):
\[
    -2\ln(1-2\phi_+) = -2\ln(1-2\bar\phi) + \frac{4}{1-2\bar\phi}\delta \phi_+ + \frac{4}{(1-2\bar\phi)^2}(\delta \phi_+)^2  + \mathcal{O}((\delta \phi_+)^3)
\]
Here we already drop the \(\delta\phi_+^3\) terms since we will project onto \(\sech^2,\sech^4\)-space later on. 
Inserting this into the EL equation for \(\phi_+\) yields:
\[
\begin{aligned}
    &\frac{2}{\bar\phi^*}\Delta \delta \phi_+ =2\left(\ln\bar\phi - \ln(1-2\bar\phi)-\mu\right) -\frac{2}{\bar\phi^*} \delta \phi_+ +  2\left(\frac{1}{\bar\phi}+\frac{2}{1-2\bar\phi}\right) \delta \phi_+ \\
    &- \left(\frac{1}{\bar\phi^2} - \frac{4}{(1-2\bar\phi)^2}\right)(\delta \phi_+)^2 - \frac{1}{\bar\phi^2}\phi_-^2 + \frac{2}{\bar\phi^3}\delta\phi_+(\phi_-)^2-\frac{1}{2\bar\phi^4}\phi_-^4\\
    \Rightarrow\quad&\Delta \delta \phi_+ = \left(\frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi(1-2\bar\phi)}-1\right) \delta \phi_+ - \left(\frac{\bar\phi^*(1-4\bar\phi)}{2\bar\phi^2(1-2\bar\phi)^2}\right)(\delta \phi_+)^2\\
    &- \frac{\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi^2}\phi_-^2 + \frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi^3}\delta\phi_+(\phi_-)^2-\frac{\bar\phi^*}{4\bar\phi^4}\phi_-^4\\
\end{aligned}
\]
Note that we ignore constants as they cancel with the chemical potential constants \(\mu_1+\mu+2\). Also we drop the \(\sech^6\)-term from the last term since it will not contribute to the final result.
Again inserting the ansatze for \(\delta \phi_+\) and \(\phi_-\) we get for the LHS:
\[
    \Delta \delta \phi_+ = 4k^2m \sech^2(kx) - 6 k^2 m \sech^4(kx)
\]
For the RHS we get
\[
   \begin{aligned}
    &\left(\frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi(1-2\bar\phi)}-1\right) m \sech^2(kx) - \left(\frac{\bar\phi^*(1-4\bar\phi)}{2\bar\phi^2(1-2\bar\phi)^2}\right)m^2 \sech^4(kx) \\
    &- \frac{\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi^2} a^2 (1-\sech^2(kx)) + \frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi^3} m a^2 \sech^2(kx) (1-\sech^2(kx))\\
    &- \frac{\bar\phi^*}{4\bar\phi^4}a^4 (1-\sech^2(kx))^2 \\
    =&-\frac{\bar\phi^*a^2}{2\bar\phi^2}\left(1+\frac{a^2}{\bar\phi^2}\right)+\left[\left(\frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi(1-2\bar\phi)}-1\right)m +\frac{\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi^2}a^2 + \frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi^3} m a^2 +\frac{\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi^4}a^4 \right] \sech^2(kx) \\
    &- \left( \frac{\bar\phi^*(1-4\bar\phi)}{2\bar\phi^2(1-2\bar\phi)^2}m^2 + \frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi^3} ma^2+\frac{\bar\phi^*}{4\bar\phi^4}a^4 \right)\sech^4(kx)
   \end{aligned}
\]
Comparing the coefficients we get:
\[
\begin{aligned}
    4k^2m &= \left(\frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi(1-2\bar\phi)}-1\right)m +\frac{\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi^2}a^2 + \frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi^3} m a^2 +\frac{\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi^4}a^4\\
    &=  \frac{\bar\phi^*(1-4\bar\phi)}{3\bar\phi^2(1-2\bar\phi)^2}m^2 + \frac{2\bar\phi^*}{3\bar\phi^3} ma^2+\frac{\bar\phi^*}{6\bar\phi^4}a^4\\
\end{aligned}
\]
Inserting the definition for \(a\) and rearranging in orders of \(m\) yields:
\[
    \begin{aligned}
        & m^2\frac{\bar\phi^*(1-4\bar\phi)}{3\bar\phi^2(1-2\bar\phi)^2}+m\left[-\frac{\bar\phi^*}{3\bar\phi^3}a^2+1-\frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi(1-2\bar\phi)}\right] - \frac{\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi^2}a^2\left(1+\frac{2a^2}{3\bar\phi^2}\right)\\
        =&m^2\frac{\bar\phi^*(1-4\bar\phi)}{3\bar\phi^2(1-2\bar\phi)^2}+m\left[-\frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi_c\bar\phi}(\bar\phi-\bar\phi_c)+1-\frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi(1-2\bar\phi)}\right] - \frac{3\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi_c}(\bar\phi-\bar\phi_c)\left(1+\frac{2}{\bar\phi_c}(\bar\phi-\bar\phi_c)\right)\\
    \end{aligned}
\]
Lastly we will compute the residual 
\[

        R =& \text{LHS} - \text{RHS} = \left[4k^2m-\left(\frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi(1-2\bar\phi)}-1\right)m +\frac{\bar\phi^*}{2\bar\phi^2}a^2 + \frac{\bar\phi^*}{\bar\phi^3} m a^2 \right]\sech^2(kx)\\

    \end{aligned}

and set its dot product with \(\sech^2(kx)\).
